Preoperative precautions include the following six points.
1. Make sure to get enough sleep the day before surgery. It is important to ensure that both the body and eyes are well rested before surgery, as this helps ensure the smooth progress of the procedure.
2. Please prepare all surgical documents and your National Health Insurance card. On the day of surgery, remember to bring your National Health Insurance card, consent form, and other relevant documents. This will help medical staff handle the procedures more smoothly, making the entire process more efficient and avoiding the hassle of searching for documents at the last minute.
3. On the day of surgery, please wear light clothing. Choose loose, easy-to-take clothing to make surgery and changing clothes more convenient and make you feel more comfortable.
4. Do not wear any accessories before surgery. This includes necklaces, earrings, bracelets, etc. This helps avoid inconvenience during the procedure and ensures safety and smooth operation.
5. Do not touch the area casually during surgery. During surgery, do not touch areas that have already been disinfected casually. This is because the nurse has carefully disinfected the area beforehand. If you touch it during the procedure, you will need to disinfect it again. This not only increases the risk of infection but also prolongs the surgery time, so please be patient and avoid touching it.
6. After surgery, please take a rest for a while and then go to the medicine counter, where the staff will prepare your post-surgery medication and carefully remind you of postoperative care precautions so you know how to take care of yourself.

The following eight points should be noted for postoperative care.
1. When covering your eyes with gauze, you don't need to apply eye drops. On the day of surgery, your eyes will be covered with gauze, so you do not need to apply eye drops that day. Please start using them at the time indicated by your doctor. 
2. For follow-ups, bring the medications prescribed on the day of surgery. When you return for your follow-up the day after surgery, please bring the medications prescribed by your doctor for the surgery, such as medication, ointment, eye mask, or care kit. After your doctor examines you, start using eye drops as instructed. 
3. Photophobia, tearing, and mild localized tingling are normal phenomena. If you feel sensitivity to light, tearing, or mild stinging in your eyes after surgery, these are actually normal postoperative symptoms. As long as you follow your doctor's advice, these symptoms usually improve gradually, and there is no need to worry excessively.
4. If you feel unwell, return for a visit immediately or go to the emergency room for consultation. If you experience sudden and significant vision loss, abnormal pain, redness, heavy bleeding, or abnormal discharge after surgery, be sure to return for medical attention immediately or go to the emergency room.
5. Try to rest in bed and avoid dangerous movements. On the day of surgery, try to rest in bed and avoid any dangerous movements that may put pressure on the eyes, such as coughing forcefully, bending over, lifting heavy objects, or strenuous exercise, to prevent affecting wound healing. Point 6. Maintain a regular lifestyle and take your medication on time. Please maintain a regular daily routine after surgery, keep your daily routine stable, avoid overexertion, and take all prescribed medications on time as directed by your doctor. 
7. Do not get water into your eyes, avoid swimming, or strenuous exercise for one month. For one month after surgery, avoid exposing your eyes to moisture, and refrain from swimming and any strenuous exercise to ensure eye safety.
8. Wear an eye mask for a month, both day and night. For one month after surgery, please wear the eye mask all day every day, especially during sleep, to ensure eye safety and protection.

Frequently Asked Questions:
1. What is cataract? 
A cataract occurs when the crystalline lens inside the eye becomes cloudy due to aging or other factors, resulting in blurred vision. It is similar to a camera lens fogging up; if light cannot easily pass through, the resulting image will not be clear. 

2. What is the difference between cataracts and myopia? 
Myopia is an issue with the shape of the eyeball or the curvature of the cornea, which makes distant objects appear out of focus. Cataracts, on the other hand, involve a clouding of the eye's lens that blurs vision at any distance, both near and far. Cataracts are also frequently accompanied by light sensitivity and a darkening or yellowing of your overall vision.

3. What are the symptoms of cataracts? 
The most common symptoms include gradually blurring vision, sensitivity to light, especially in strong sunlight, worsening night vision, and colors appearing yellowed or darkened. Patients may also experience a sudden, rapid shift in their glasses prescription as the condition deepens. 

4. I've had to change my glasses frequently lately. Could this be cataracts? 
Yes, it is possible. Early-stage cataracts can alter how the crystalline lens refracts light, causing a sudden progression of nearsightedness. It is highly recommended that you visit an ophthalmologist for a dilated eye exam so a doctor can formally evaluate your condition.

5. Why do cataracts make people sensitive to light? 
A cloudy crystalline lens causes incoming light to scatter rather than focus cleanly. This scattering is especially noticeable in bright environments, creating an uncomfortable glaring effect that leads to photophobia. 

6. Can cataracts cause complete blindness? 
If left completely untreated, severe cataracts can eventually lead to secondary complications like glaucoma or severe inflammation, which can permanently affect your vision. However, with timely surgical intervention, the vast majority of patients recover their vision well and do not go blind. 

7. Can cataracts occur in both eyes? 
Usually, cataracts will develop in both eyes, though the severity and speed of progression can vary significantly between them. Your doctor will evaluate each eye individually to determine the best timing for surgery. 

8. Are cataracts only common in elderly people? 
While aging is the most frequent cause—making cataracts most common among middle-aged and elderly individuals—younger people can also develop them. Risk factors for early-onset cataracts include high myopia, diabetes, eye trauma, and long-term use of steroids. 

9. At what level of myopia does the risk of developing cataracts increase? 
Generally, high myopia—typically defined as a prescription worse than -6.00 diopters—is a significant risk factor for cataracts. The more severe the myopia, the greater the risk of premature aging in the eye's crystalline lens. 

10. Is there a relationship between diabetes and cataracts? 
Yes. If blood sugar is poorly controlled over a long period, it accelerates the breakdown of proteins within the crystalline lens. Because of this, patients with diabetes tend to develop cataracts earlier and see them progress faster—often developing them about ten years earlier than the average person. 

11. Can long-term use of steroid eye drops cause cataracts? 
Yes. Long-term use of steroid eye drops, oral corticosteroids, or injectable steroids significantly increases the risk of developing posterior subcapsular cataracts. Regular eye exams are required if you use these medications.

12. Does sun exposure cause cataracts? 
Long-term exposure to ultraviolet rays does indeed increase your risk of developing cataracts. It is strongly recommended to wear UV-blocking sunglasses, say, UV400, during outdoor activities to protect your eyes. 

13. Can an eye injury cause cataracts? 
Yes. Trauma, such as a blunt impact or puncture wound, can directly damage the crystalline lens and lead to a traumatic cataract. These can occur at a young age and are often more surgically complex to treat.

14. Can cataracts be prevented? 
While age-related cataracts cannot be completely prevented, you can significantly reduce your risk and slow their progression. Preventative steps include quitting smoking, tightly controlling blood sugar, wearing UV-protective sunglasses outdoors, avoiding prolonged and unnecessary steroid use, and maintaining a balanced diet.

15. Are cataracts and glaucoma the same thing? 
No, they are completely different conditions. A cataract is the clouding of the eye's lens and can be successfully treated with surgery. Glaucoma involves damage to the optic nerve; any vision lost to glaucoma is irreversible. However, it is possible for a patient to have both conditions at the same time. 

16. What is the difference between cataracts and macular degeneration? 
Cataracts are problems with the crystalline lens at the front of the eye; Macular degeneration is a problem in the posterior central retina. Both affect vision, but their causes and treatments are completely different. 

17. I am seeing double. Could this be caused by cataracts?
It is definitely possible. Cataracts can cause double or blurred vision in just one eye. However, seeing double can also stem from corneal problems or misaligned eyes, such as strabismus. You will need a comprehensive eye exam so a doctor can determine the exact cause. 

18. If my cataracts are not severe in the early stages, do they need immediate treatment?
Mild cataracts can simply be observed and followed up regularly. However, if the condition begins to affect your daily life, work, or driving safety, you can evaluate the option for surgery without waiting for it to become "very serious." 

19. Is surgery absolutely necessary for cataracts? Are there any alternative methods?
In modern medicine, surgery is the only effective treatment for cataracts. Eye drops or medications can only slightly slow their progression; they cannot make a cloudy crystalline lens clear again. 

20. I heard that you must wait until cataracts are "fully mature" before having surgery. Is that true?
That is an outdated concept. With today's advanced technology, surgery can be performed as soon as the cataract begins to affect your daily life. In fact, waiting until a cataract is overmature makes the surgery more difficult and increases the risks. 

21. Can eye drops cure cataracts?
There are no eye drops proven to cure cataracts. Surgery is the only effective method; do not delay and miss the optimal time for surgery just because you are relying on eye drops. 

22. I've noticed that things look yellow. Is this caused by cataracts?
Yes. Cataracts cause the crystalline lens to turn yellowish-brown, which gives everything a warm, yellowish tint. This makes it especially hard to distinguish between blue and purple hues. 

23. Can cataracts come back after surgery?
True cataracts do not recur after surgery. However, a small percentage of people may develop a "secondary cataract" (posterior capsule opacification) months or years later, which causes postoperative cloudiness. This can be easily treated with a quick outpatient laser procedure. 

24. How do you determine the severity of cataracts?
Cataracts can be roughly divided into four stages. 1. Mild: The effects on vision are minimal. 2. Moderate: There is obvious photophobia (light sensitivity) and glare. 3. Mature: Patients experience a significant decline in vision. 4. Hypermature (overripe): The lens begins to liquefy, making surgery much more difficult. 

25. Do cataracts cause pain?
Cataracts themselves usually do not cause any pain. If you are experiencing eye pain, it may be due to other serious issues, such as acute glaucoma, and you should seek medical attention promptly. 

26. Do cataracts progress quickly?
The rate of progression varies from person to person. Age-related cataracts typically progress very slowly, sometimes taking several years. However, cataracts caused by diabetes, trauma, or steroid use tend to progress much more rapidly. 

27. Can cataracts be resolved by simply changing my glasses?
While changing your prescription may provide temporary improvement in the early stages, it cannot correct the light scattering caused by worsening cataracts. Eventually, surgery will be required. 

28. My family member had excellent vision after surgery. Will I have the same result?
Most people experience a significant improvement in their vision following surgery. However, if you have other underlying eye issues—such as glaucoma or macular degeneration—your results may be limited. Your doctor will assess your expected outcome prior to the procedure. 

29. Will my degree of myopia (nearsightedness) increase after cataract surgery?
The prescription of the implanted artificial lens is fixed and will not change. However, if you are younger and the axial length of your eye is still changing, there may be slight shifts in your prescription over time. 

30. Are cataracts caused by the eye's lens dissolving?
No. A cataract is actually the clouding and breakdown of the proteins inside the eye's natural lens. While a severely advanced (overmature) cataract can begin to liquefy, most cataracts harden. Modern cataract surgery uses ultrasound to gently break up the clouded lens so it can be safely suctioned out. 

31. Are cataracts hereditary?
Age-related cataracts do not have strong hereditary tendencies. However, congenital cataracts (present at birth) do have a partial genetic link. 

32. Will cataract surgery cure my presbyopia (need for reading glasses)?
Not necessarily. If you choose a standard monofocal lens, it will not correct presbyopia, and you will still need reading glasses for near vision. To address presbyopia at the same time, you would need to choose a multifocal or extended depth-of-focus (EDOF) artificial lens. 

33. Is 50 years old too young to have cataract surgery?
Age is not the deciding factor; the impact on your vision is. If cataracts are significantly affecting your quality of life, surgery can be considered regardless of your age. 

34. Can cataracts be seen from the outside?
Mild cataracts are not noticeable to others. However, in severe cases, the pupil may appear to have a white or gray cloudiness. 

35. Are cataracts and presbyopia the same thing?
No, they are different. Presbyopia is the loss of elasticity in the lens, which only affects your close-range vision. Cataracts involve a clouding of the lens that blurs vision at all distances and is often accompanied by light sensitivity and changes in color perception. 

36. What is a congenital cataract?
Congenital cataracts are present in infants or young children and may be linked to genetics, infections, or metabolic diseases. Early treatment is crucial to prevent the development of amblyopia (lazy eye). 

37. Is it normal for cataracts to make lighting seem extremely glaring?
Yes, this is completely normal. This is a typical symptom of photophobia and glare caused by light scattering as it passes through the cloudy lens. Patients usually see significant improvement after surgery. 

38. Does smoking affect cataracts?
Yes, it does. Smoking accelerates oxidation and protein degeneration within the lens, causing cataracts to develop earlier and progress much faster. Quitting smoking is highly beneficial for your eye health. 

39. Can the need for cataract surgery be determined just by looking at my visual acuity score (e.g., 20/40 or 0.5)?
Not exclusively. Some people have decent numerical visual acuity but experience severe glare or poor night vision that significantly affects their lives. Your doctor will evaluate multiple factors to determine the best timing for surgery. 

40. Can cataracts be improved through diet?
There is no food that can cure or reverse already-developed cataracts. While antioxidants like lutein and vitamins C and E can help slow down the aging of the eyes, they cannot replace the need for surgery. 

41. After cataract surgery, my floaters have become more obvious. Why is that?
Because the cloudy natural lens has been replaced with a crystal-clear artificial lens, more light enters your eye. This increased light makes the floaters that were already present much more noticeable. 

42. Can cataract surgery be performed on both eyes at the same time?
Most hospitals in Taiwan recommend performing the surgeries separately, typically with a 1-to-2-week interval. This is primarily for safety, to prevent the rare risk of simultaneous infection or complications in both eyes. 

43. Is fasting necessary before cataract surgery?
Because the surgery is usually performed under local anesthesia, fasting is generally not required. However, please follow the specific instructions on your procedure notification, and consult your doctor if you have special medical circumstances. 

44. Is the success rate of cataract surgery high?
Yes. Cataract surgery is one of the most widely performed and technically mature procedures worldwide, boasting an overall success rate exceeding 95%. Most patients experience significantly improved outcomes after surgery.

45. How long after cataract surgery will I be able to see clearly?
Vision typically starts to improve within a few days of the procedure. Most patients see significant progress within 1 to 2 weeks, while complete visual stability takes about 1 to 3 months. 

46. Can I fly on an airplane after cataract surgery?
It is generally recommended to wait two weeks after surgery, once the wound has stabilized, before taking a flight. Because the dry air in the cabin can worsen dry eyes, remember to apply artificial tears frequently during the flight. 

47. Can cataracts cause headaches?
Cataracts themselves do not directly cause headaches. However, if a cataract becomes overmature and triggers glaucoma, it can lead to headaches, nausea, and vomiting, which requires urgent medical attention. 

48. Why do I need so many tests before cataract surgery?
These pre-surgery exams give your doctor a complete picture of your overall eye health. They allow the medical team to accurately calculate the prescription for your new artificial lens and evaluate your cornea, retina, and eye pressure, ensuring the surgery is as safe and effective as possible. 

49. Will my eyes look different after cataract surgery?
To other people, your eyes will look exactly the same. The artificial lens is placed deep inside your eye and is invisible from the outside. If you had a very advanced cataract that made your pupil look white, the pupil will simply return to its normal black color after the surgery. 

50. Can cataracts be treated with just lasers instead of surgery? 
No. Lasers (such as femtosecond lasers) are simply tools used to assist the surgeon during the procedure, not a standalone treatment. To truly remove the cloudy lens, traditional surgical techniques are still required.

51. Question: I am already 80 years old. Is it still suitable for cataract surgery? 
A: Age is not the issue; physical condition is the key. As long as there is no severe systemic illness that makes surgery untolerable, many elderly people aged 80 to 90 see significant improvement in vision after surgery. 

52. Q: Will cataract surgery fix my existing astigmatism? 
Answer: Standard cataract surgery does not correct astigmatism, meaning you will still have it after the procedure. However, if you want to fix your astigmatism at the same time as your cataract removal, you can choose a special astigmatism-correcting artificial lens, often called a toric lens.

53. Q: Can cataracts suddenly worsen? 
Answer: Usually, it doesn't suddenly worsen but gradually worsens. However, trauma or certain medications may cause cataracts to worsen rapidly. If vision significantly declines in the short term, medical attention should be sought promptly. 

54. Q: After cataract surgery, do I still need some dilating agents? 
Answer: Routine dripping of mydriatic agents is not required after surgery. Only during post-surgery check-ups or in specific cases will the doctor ask you to apply a mydriatic to examine the fundus. 

55. Q: After cataract surgery, can I donate my cornea? 
Answer: Yes, you can. Cataract surgery does not affect corneal donation because the artificial lens remains inside the eye, and the corneal tissue remains intact. 

56. Question: Can cataracts heal on their own? 
Answer: No, it won't.  Once cataracts form, they cannot restore transparency on their own and will only worsen over time. Hand techniques are the only effective treatment method. 

57. Question: I have high blood pressure. Can I get cataracts? 
Answer: Yes, you can. As long as blood pressure is kept within a stable range, cataract surgery is safe. Please take your medication on time before surgery, and you can also take blood  pressure medication as usual on the day of the procedure. 

58. Question: Can I cry after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, you can. Tears do not harm the eyes. However, in the early postoperative stage, try not to rub your eyes forcefully. If you cry a lot, gently press the corners with a clean tissue to fix it. 

59. Q: After cataract surgery, will my eyes become very sensitive? 
Answer: In the early postoperative stage, you may experience sensitivity such as photophobia or foreign body sensation, which is normal. As the wound heals and the eye adapts, sensitivity gradually returns to normal. 

60. Q: Can I dye my hair after cataract surgery? 
Answer: It is recommended to dye your hair no later than 2-4 weeks after the procedure. The chemicals in hair dye may irritate the eyes, and water or chemicals may flow into the eyes during dyeing, increasing the risk of infection. 

61. Question: Can I soak in hot springs after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Not recommended. Hot spring water contains more bacteria, and high temperatures may affect wound healing. It is recommended to wait at least 1-2 months after surgery, and only consider after the doctor confirms the wound has fully healed. 

62. Q: Can I keep pets after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, but in the early postoperative stage, be careful to prevent pet hair and dust from getting into your eyes to prevent infection. After touching pets, be sure to wash your hands thoroughly, apply medication, or touch your eyes. 

63. Question: Can eyelash extensions be implanted after cataract surgery? 
Answer: It is recommended to wait at least one month until the wound has fully healed and the eye is stable, before considering it. Glue and tools for eyelash extensions may irritate the eyes, so be sure to choose a store with good hygiene quality. 

64. Q: Can I wear contact lenses after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Generally, it is not necessary, because artificial crystalline lenses have replaced the functions of crystalline lenses. If there are special needs (such as residual orthodontic prescriptions), the mask must be evaluated by a physician before wearing it. 

65. Q: Can I play golf after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, but wait until the wound stabilizes. It is recommended to wait at least one month after surgery, and then gradually recover after confirmation by your doctor. In the early stages, avoid swinging too hard to prevent increased intraocular pressure. 

66. Question: Can I do housework after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Light household chores (such as folding clothes or washing dishes) can be done a few days after surgery. However, avoid activities that increase eye pressure, such as bending over or lowering your head, lifting heavy objects, or mopping the floor, for at least 2-4 weeks. 

67. Question: After cataract surgery, can I hold grandchildren? 
A: Yes, but be careful not to let your grandchild's hand or head hit your eye. In the early postoperative period, avoid lifting heavy objects (including carrying heavier infants). It is recommended to hold them again 2-4 weeks after surgery. 

68. Question: Can I sing after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, you can. Singing does not affect the eyes. However, if you hold your breath or  use too much force while singing, it's best to pay a little attention in the early postoperative period to avoid a sudden increase in intraocular pressure. 

69. Question: Can I eat seafood after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, you can. Unless you are allergic to seafood, there are no contraindications. Seafood is rich in egg white, which in turn helps wound healing. 

70. Q: Can I drink coffee or tea after cataract surgery? 
Answer: It can be consumed in moderation. Caffeine does not directly affect wounds, but drinking too much can cause insomnia or palpitations, indirectly affecting rest. Just enough to moderate. 

71. Question: Can I take Chinese medicine for tonic supplementation after cataract surgery? 
Answer: It is recommended to consult a doctor first. Certain Chinese medicines that promote blood circulation and remove stasis (such as angelica, ginseng, and ginkgo) may increase the risk of bleeding and should be avoided in the early postoperative stage. 

72. Question: After cataract surgery, can I eat ginger duck or spicy hot pot? 
Answer: It is recommended to avoid overly spicy and irritating foods for 1-2 weeks after surgery to prevent inflammation or gastrointestinal discomfort. A mild diet is better for recovery. 

73. Question: After cataract surgery, can I eat ice or drink cold beverages? 
Answer: Yes, you can. There is no evidence that cold diets affect eye recovery. As long as your stomach can tolerate it, drinking it in moderation is fine. 

74. Q: Can I smoke after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Strongly recommend not. Smoking delays wound healing, increases the risk of infection, and is harmful to both your eyes and overall health. This is a good time to quit smoking. 

75. Question: Can I drink alcohol after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Avoid drinking alcohol for up to 2 weeks after surgery. Alcohol may affect the effectiveness of the medication and increase the risk of bleeding. Drinking small amounts afterward is usually not a big problem, but it is recommended to consult a doctor first. 

76. Question: Can I have sex after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, but avoid overly vigorous movements and holding your breath to prevent increased intraocular pressure. It is recommended to proceed gently within 1-2 weeks after surgery. 

77. Q: After cataract surgery, can I massage around the eyes? 
Answer: No, it cannot. Do not press or massage around the eyes for at least 1-2 months after surgery to avoid affecting the wound or causing the artificial lens to shift. 

78. Q: After cataract surgery, can I use a facial washer or an infusion device? 
Answer: Not recommended. In the early postoperative stage, avoid any vibrations or pressure around the eyes. Wait at least 1-2 months before using it after confirmation by your doctor. 

79. Question: After cataract surgery, can eye masks or eye creams be applied? 
Answer: It is recommended to wait until the wound has fully healed (about one month) before using it. When applying the eye mask, avoid the actual eyeball and make sure the product does not flow into the eyes. 

80. Question: Can I wear makeup after cataract surgery? 
Answer: Other areas of the face (base makeup) are fine, but avoid the eye area. For eye makeup (eyeshadow, mascara, eyeliner), it is recommended to wait at least 4 weeks before starting with your doctor's approval. 

81. Q: How is cataract surgery performed? 
Answer: Today, the most common method is ultrasound surgery, also called phacoemulsification. First, numbing drops are applied to the eye. The surgeon then makes a tiny incision—about 2 to 3 millimeters—and uses ultrasonic vibration to gently break up and suction out the cloudy natural lens. Finally, a clear artificial lens is implanted in its place. Because the incision is so small, it usually heals on its own without needing stitches.

82. Q: Does cataract surgery require general anesthesia? 
Answer: No, it is not. Usually, just local anesthesia and applying anesthetic eye drops are enough. The whole process keeps you awake but not painful; the procedure lasts about 15-30 minutes. 

83. Question: My eyes move around during the procedure. What should the doctor do? 
A: The doctor will use an eyelid opener to secure your eyelids. Slight eye movement is normal; please relax and not be nervous; the procedure will go more smoothly. 

84. Q: What will I see during the hand technique? Is it really scary? 
A: You may see the strong light of the hand lamp and some blurry colors, but you won't see a clear image of the hand instruments. Most people think it's okay, not scary. 

85. Q: How long does the technique take? 
Answer: Manual hand techniques take about 15-30 minutes. Including preoperative preparation and postoperative observation, the entire process takes about 2-3 hours. 

86. Question: If the wound is small and doesn't require stitches, won't it split open? 
Answer: No, it won't. Modern hand techniques use a 'self-sealing' design, which automatically seals the body. As long as you don't rub your eyes or get wet, the wound heals very well. 

87. Q: What is femtosecond laser-assisted cataract surgery? 
Answer: This is done before traditional ultrasound techniques, where CT is first used for localization, then femtosecond lasers are used to precisely create incisions, tear open the pouches, and break up the crystalline lenses. This is an advanced option with self-payment. 

88. Question: What makes femtosecond laser cataract surgery better than traditional procedures? 
Answer: There are three main advantages. First, the laser softens the cataract beforehand, meaning less ultrasound energy is needed. This helps protect the delicate cells inside your cornea. Second, the laser makes a perfectly circular opening in the capsule, the pouch holding the lens, allowing your new premium artificial lens to be centered with perfect precision. Third, it lowers the overall risk of complications, especially for more advanced or difficult cataracts.

89. Question: How much does a femtosecond laser procedure cost? Is it covered by National Health Insurance? 
Answer: Femtosecond laser is a self-paid item and is not covered by National Health Insurance. Costs vary by clinic, with a single glance costing tens of thousands of NTD. Please consult the hospital for detailed pricing. 

90. Q: Who is suitable for femtosecond laser cataract surgery? 
Answer: Those with overmature or overly hard cataracts, few corneal endothelial cells, astigmatism requiring fine correction, or those choosing advanced multifocal crystalline lenses can all consider this. 

91. Question: Does National Health Insurance cover cataract surgery? 
Answer: Yes, there is. Standard ultrasonic emulsification procedures and basic NHI crystalline lenses are covered by NHI. Only upgrading to crystalline lenses or femtosecond lasers requires personal payment. 

92. Q: Is hand surgery inpatient or outpatient? 
Answer: Most can be done in outpatient hand surgery rooms and do not require hospitalization. After surgery, you can go home after 1-2 hours of observation. 

93. Q: Can cataracts in both eyes be performed on the same procedure? 
Answer: Most hospitals in Taiwan recommend separate procedures, with intervals of 1-2 weeks. The main reason is safety, to prevent simultaneous infection in both eyes. 

94. Q: What examinations are needed before hand surgery? 
Answer: This includes visual acuity, intraocular pressure, corneal topography, corneal endothelial cell count, axial length measurement, and dilated fundus examination. Ensure the safety of the handicrafts. 

95. Q: Is the success rate of cataract surgery high? 
Answer: Very high, over 95%. This is one of the most widely practiced techniques worldwide, with highly mature technology. 

96. Question: How long after cataract surgery can you see clearly? 
A: Usually, improvement begins within a few days, with significant progress within 1-2 weeks, and complete stability takes about 1-3 months. 

97. Q: How much does it cost to have a single eye surgery for cataract surgery? 
Answer: If you choose the National Health Insurance standard manual surgery plus NHI crystalline lenses, the deductible is very low. If you choose self-paid crystalline lenses, the cost can reach tens of thousands at first glance. Please consult the hospital. 

98. Question: Can I go home on the same day after the procedure? 
Answer: No, it cannot. After surgery, her vision temporarily blurred, and with the effect of pupil dilation, she must be accompanied by a family member. Do not drive or ride a motorcycle by herself. 

99. Question: Cataract surgery uses ultrasonic waves to 'blast' the crystalline lenses. Will it not harm the eyes? 
Answer: Ultrasound energy is precisely controlled and targets only crystalline lenses. Modern hand techniques are well-designed and highly secure. 

100. Q: Is fasting required before surgery? 
Answer: Because it is local anesthesia, fasting is generally not necessary. Please follow the instructions on the procedure notification slip, and consult your doctor in advance if you have any questions. 

101. Q: Which is better, femtosecond laser or traditional hand techniques? 
Answer: Both are safe and suitable for different situations. Traditional hand techniques are suitable for most people; Femtosecond laser is suitable for patients with special needs or difficult cataracts. 

102. Question: Can I request to stop during a hand technique? 
Answer: Not recommended.  Once the technique begins, stopping midway can cause even more problems. Thorough communication and relaxation before surgery are very important. 

103. Question: What should I do if I accidentally blink or move during hand surgery? 
Answer: The doctor will use a blister to fix the eyelid. Please relax, breathe calmly, and avoid sudden and large head-shifts. 

104. Question: How long is the interval between eye surgery? 
Answer: It is usually recommended to wait 1-2 weeks for the first eye to recover initially before proceeding to the second eye. 

105. Question: The doctor said it was difficult for me to have cataract surgery. Do I need to take that risk? 
Answer: The doctor tells you so you can prepare mentally, but it doesn't necessarily mean there's a problem. Difficult cataracts can still be successfully completed; please trust the medical team. 

106. Q: Is hospitalization required for cataract surgery? How many days does it take? 
Answer: Most are outpatient day procedures, and hospitalization is not required. Complex cases may require a one-day hospital stay for observation. 

107. Question: Is cataract surgery commonly seen in ophthalmology clinics? 
A: Yes, this is one of the most widely performed surgical procedures worldwide, and also the most commonly seen in ophthalmology in Taiwan. 

108. Q: Is the recovery time the same for femtosecond laser techniques and traditional techniques? 
Answer: The recovery times for both are similar.  Because femtosecond laser reduces ultrasound energy, some patients may experience slightly faster postoperative vision recovery. 

109. Q: Do I need to shave my eyebrows or head for cataract surgery? 
Answer: No, it is not.  The procedure is performed locally around the eyes and does not require shaving eyebrows or hair. 

110. Q: Is cataract surgery performed in a completely dark environment? 
Answer: No.  The procedure is performed under a hand microscope, illuminated by strong light so the doctor can see clearly. 

111. Question: Does cataract surgery hurt?

500. Question: After cataract surgery, how should I take care of my eyes to maintain my vision longer? 
Answer: Regular follow-up visits, chronic diseases (blood sugar, blood pressure), wearing UV-protective sunglasses outdoors, a balanced diet (lutein, vitamin C and E), no smoking, and avoiding eye injuries. Most importantly, seek medical attention as soon as possible if there are any changes in vision.
